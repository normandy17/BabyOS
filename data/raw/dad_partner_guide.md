# Dad & Partner Complete Pregnancy Guide

## Your Role During Pregnancy

Being a supportive partner during pregnancy is one of the most important things you can do for your growing family. Research consistently shows that involved partners lead to better birth outcomes, lower rates of postnatal depression in mothers, and stronger early parent-child bonds.

This guide is written specifically for dads and non-birthing partners — including those who are new to the country, new to the healthcare system, and navigating all of this while building a life somewhere unfamiliar.

---

## First Trimester (Weeks 1–12): The Invisible Hard Part

### What she is going through:
- Extreme fatigue — progesterone causes tiredness that feels like illness
- Nausea that can be constant and debilitating, not just morning-based
- Heightened sense of smell — cooking odours, perfumes, and cleaning products may trigger vomiting
- Emotional turbulence — joy, fear, anxiety, and hormonal mood swings simultaneously
- Breast tenderness — sometimes severe
- Frequent urination
- Food aversions to previously loved foods
- All of this while the pregnancy may not yet be visible to anyone

### What you can do:
- Take over cooking, or at minimum keep strong cooking smells out of the house (use the extractor fan, cook outside if possible)
- Keep plain crackers, ginger biscuits, or dry toast available at all times
- Handle strong-smelling household tasks: bins, cleaning products, fish
- Do not comment on food choices or aversions — she is not being difficult
- Accompany her to the first doctor's appointment and the first scan
- Let her rest without guilt — fatigue in the first trimester is biological, not laziness
- Do not tell her to "push through" or compare to other pregnancies
- Ask "what do you need right now?" rather than assuming

### Emotional support:
- The first trimester carries the highest risk of miscarriage. She may be holding both excitement and fear simultaneously.
- Do not rush her to tell people. Follow her lead on timing.
- If she seems withdrawn or anxious, this is normal. Create space for her to talk without pressure.

### Practical tasks for you this trimester:
- [ ] Research and book the first Frauenarzt (gynaecologist) appointment if not yet done
- [ ] Start looking for a Hebamme (midwife) — the waiting lists are long in Germany
- [ ] Research your health insurance (Krankenkasse) and what is covered
- [ ] Read about the Mutterpass — understand what it is before the appointment
- [ ] Begin researching hospitals and birth centres in your area

## Second Trimester (Weeks 13–27): The Honeymoon Phase

### What she is going through:
- Energy typically returns after week 14
- Nausea usually eases (though not always)
- The bump becomes visible — the pregnancy becomes real to others
- First fetal movements felt (quickening) — usually weeks 16–22 for first-time mothers
- Back pain begins as the uterus grows
- Round ligament pain — sharp twinges in the lower abdomen when she moves suddenly
- Increasing emotional connection with the baby
- Body image changes as the bump grows

### What you can do:
- Go to the 20-week anatomy scan — this is the most detailed ultrasound and a major milestone
- You may find out the sex of the baby at this scan (if you want to)
- Talk to the bump — the baby begins hearing around week 16–18. Your voice matters.
- Help with back pain: offer massages, suggest a pregnancy pillow, look into a Stützgürtel (maternity support belt)
- Plan travel or holidays now — the second trimester is the safest and most comfortable time to travel
- Begin discussing birth preferences: hospital vs. Geburtshaus, pain relief wishes, who will be present
- Attend the Geburtsvorbereitungskurs (birth preparation course) together

### Your own emotional experience:
- It is entirely normal to feel disconnected or less "real" about the pregnancy than she does — you are not experiencing the physical sensations
- Anxiety about finances, fatherhood readiness, and the future is universal
- Talk to other fathers. Find expat parent groups in your city.
- In Göttingen: look for international parent groups, the university hospital (UMG — Universitätsmedizin Göttingen) has English-speaking staff

### Practical tasks for you this trimester:
- [ ] Attend the 20-week scan together
- [ ] Start the Geburtsvorbereitungskurs (book early, they fill up)
- [ ] Visit and register at the Kreißsaal (delivery ward) of your chosen hospital
- [ ] Research Elterngeld — understand your entitlement before baby arrives
- [ ] Begin assembling the nursery
- [ ] Start the hospital bag checklist

## Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40): Preparation Mode

### What she is going through:
- Significant physical discomfort: back pain, pelvic girdle pain, shortness of breath, insomnia
- Braxton Hicks contractions intensifying
- Emotional intensity — anxiety about birth, excitement, fear
- The nesting instinct: urgent desire to clean, organise, and prepare the home
- Increased vulnerability and need for reassurance
- Swollen feet and ankles, especially by evening
- Difficulty with simple tasks: bending, getting up from sitting, finding a sleeping position

### What you can do:
- Do not question the nesting instinct. Support it.
- Take on all heavy lifting and physically demanding tasks without being asked
- Help her find a comfortable sleeping position — a Stillkissen (nursing/pregnancy pillow) placed between knees is often helpful
- Learn the signs of true labour vs Braxton Hicks:
  - **True labour**: regular, rhythmic contractions that get closer together, stronger, and longer; do not stop with movement or a warm bath
  - **Braxton Hicks**: irregular, do not intensify, often ease with movement or rest
- Know three routes to the hospital
- Save the Kreißsaal direct phone number in your phone
- Know where the Mutterpass is at all times
- Prepare the hospital bag together (see checklist below)

### The birth:
- Your role is to be present, calm, and supportive — not to manage or direct
- Advocate for her birth preferences with medical staff if she cannot in the moment
- Skin-to-skin contact after birth is important for the baby — ask to hold the baby skin-to-skin if she is unable to immediately
- Cutting the umbilical cord: ask if you would like to do this — it is usually offered
- Be prepared for birth to be unpredictable. Plans change. Your presence is more important than the plan.

### Practical tasks for you this trimester:
- [ ] Pack the hospital bag (both yours and hers)
- [ ] Finalise the birth plan
- [ ] Apply for Elterngeld (can do after birth, but prepare documents now)
- [ ] Register baby's Krankenkasse coverage in advance
- [ ] Arrange paternity leave with your employer
- [ ] Prepare the car seat (must be installed before leaving hospital)
- [ ] Stock the freezer with easy meals for the first weeks postpartum
- [ ] Set up the baby sleeping space (in parents' room for first 6 months is recommended)

## Hospital Bag Checklist

### For her:
- [ ] Mutterpass (essential — do not forget)
- [ ] Krankenversicherungskarte (health insurance card)
- [ ] Photo ID
- [ ] Birth plan (printed copy)
- [ ] Comfortable nightgowns / button-front for breastfeeding
- [ ] Nursing bras (x2)
- [ ] Maternity pads (thick sanitary pads, not tampons)
- [ ] Toiletries and lip balm
- [ ] Snacks and drinks for labour
- [ ] Phone charger
- [ ] Going-home outfit (maternity size still needed)
- [ ] Slippers

### For baby:
- [ ] 2–3 baby grows (Bodys) in newborn and 0–3 month sizes
- [ ] Baby hat
- [ ] Blanket
- [ ] Going-home outfit
- [ ] Car seat (installed in the car before you leave)

### For you:
- [ ] Change of clothes
- [ ] Snacks and drinks (labour can be long — hospital cafeterias have limited hours)
- [ ] Phone charger + portable battery
- [ ] Cash (some hospital car parks are cash only)
- [ ] Entertainment for waiting periods (books, tablet)
- [ ] Camera (if you want photos beyond phone quality)

## After the Birth: The Fourth Trimester

### The first weeks:
- Sleep deprivation is the defining feature — it is harder than most people imagine
- Divide night duties as much as possible — if she is breastfeeding, you can handle nappy changes, winding, and resettling
- The Hebamme will visit at home daily for the first 10 days in Germany — be present for these visits when possible
- She will experience significant hormonal changes after birth — the "baby blues" (days 3–5) are normal but watch for signs of postnatal depression that persist beyond 2 weeks

### Signs of postnatal depression to watch for:
- Persistent low mood beyond 2 weeks after birth
- Inability to bond with the baby
- Excessive anxiety or intrusive thoughts
- Feeling overwhelmed to the point of not functioning
- Not sleeping when the baby sleeps (beyond normal adjustment)
- If concerned: contact the Hebamme, GP (Hausarzt), or psychiatrist — treatment is effective and covered by insurance

### Your own mental health:
- Paternal postnatal depression affects 1 in 10 fathers — it is real and treatable
- Signs: withdrawal, irritability, feeling overwhelmed, inability to connect with the baby
- Do not dismiss your own mental health. Speak to your GP if you are struggling.

### Practical tasks after birth:
- [ ] Register the birth at the Standesamt (registry office) within one week — bring hospital birth certificate, passports, marriage certificate if applicable
- [ ] Apply for the Geburtsurkunde (birth certificate) — you will need multiple certified copies
- [ ] Apply for Kindergeld at the Familienkasse
- [ ] Apply for Elterngeld within 3 months of birth
- [ ] Add baby to health insurance (Familienversicherung)
- [ ] Apply for German residency registration (Anmeldung) for the baby
- [ ] If applicable: check the process for adding the baby to your Indian passport / OCI card

## Navigating Germany as a New Parent Without German

- **At medical appointments**: You have the right to request an interpreter. University hospitals (like UMG Göttingen) typically have staff who speak English. Bring a list of key German medical terms (see Germany Pregnancy Guide).
- **Google Translate camera**: Use it for official letters, forms, and the Mutterpass entries
- **DeepL**: Better than Google Translate for German — use it for written communications
- **Expat parent groups**: Search Facebook for "English speaking parents Göttingen" or "Internationale Eltern Göttingen"
- **Your Krankenkasse**: Most major insurers (TK, AOK, Barmer) have English-speaking customer service lines
- **The Hebamme**: Many urban midwives speak English — specifically ask for an English-speaking Hebamme when searching
