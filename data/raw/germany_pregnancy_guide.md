# Germany Pregnancy Guide for Immigrants

## The Mutterpass (Mother's Passport)

The Mutterpass is the official German pregnancy record booklet. It is issued by your Gynecologist (Frauenarzt/Frauenärztin) at your first antenatal appointment and you must carry it to every appointment, the hospital, and the birth. It contains:

- Personal data and blood type
- Blood test results (including HIV, rubella immunity, blood group, CMV)
- Urine test results at each visit
- Blood pressure and weight tracking
- Fundal height measurements
- Fetal position and heartbeat records
- Ultrasound reports
- Risk factor assessments

**Never leave the Mutterpass at home** — the delivery room (Kreißsaal) staff rely on it for your complete medical history.

## The Vorsorgeuntersuchungen (Antenatal Check-Ups)

Germany has a structured antenatal check-up schedule covered by statutory health insurance (gesetzliche Krankenversicherung / GKV). These are the standard 10+ appointments:

| Visit | Week | What Happens |
|-------|------|-------------|
| 1st | 6–10 | Pregnancy confirmed, Mutterpass issued, blood tests (blood type, rubella, HIV, hepatitis, STIs), first ultrasound |
| 2nd | ~10 | Blood pressure, urine, weight, fetal heartbeat |
| 3rd | ~12 | 1st trimester ultrasound (Ersttrimester-Screening), nuchal translucency measurement |
| 4th | ~16 | Blood, urine, weight, blood pressure, fundal height |
| 5th | ~20 | 2nd trimester ultrasound (Feindiagnostik / anomaly scan) — most detailed scan |
| 6th | ~24 | Glucose tolerance test (Gestationsdiabetes-Screening), blood pressure monitoring |
| 7th | ~28 | Blood tests (haemoglobin for anaemia), fetal position, anti-D if Rh-negative |
| 8th | ~32 | 3rd trimester ultrasound (growth scan), fetal position |
| 9th | ~36 | GBS swab, fetal position, birth preparation discussion |
| 10th | ~38 | Weekly checks begin, CTG (cardiotocography / fetal heart monitoring) |
| 11th+ | ~40 | Weekly CTG, induction discussion if overdue |

**Important**: Additional specialist ultrasounds (DEGUM-certified Feindiagnostik) can be requested and may require private payment or referral.

## Finding a Hebamme (Midwife)

A Hebamme (midwife) plays a central role in German pregnancy care. Start searching at **8–10 weeks** as demand is extremely high, especially in cities.

### Types of Hebamme care:
- **Hausgeburtshebamme**: supports home births
- **Beleghebamme**: attends your hospital birth (you hire them independently)
- **Klinkhebamme**: employed by the hospital (may not be "your" midwife)
- **Wochenbetthebamme**: postnatal care at home (covered by insurance for up to 16 weeks)

### How to find a Hebamme:
1. Ask your Frauenarzt for recommendations
2. Search at: www.hebammensuche.de
3. Ask in local Facebook expat groups (e.g. "English-speaking Göttingen")
4. Contact the nearest Geburtshaus (birth centre)

### What a Hebamme does postnatally:
- Daily home visits for the first week after birth
- Breastfeeding support
- Weight checks for the baby
- Monitoring for postnatal depression
- Belly binding and recovery advice
- Covered fully by German health insurance

## Geburtsvorbereitungskurs (Birth Preparation Course)

Both parents can attend. Covered by health insurance (for the mother; partner pays a small fee — typically €50–100 for the full course).

- Duration: usually 6–8 evening sessions or an intensive weekend
- Topics: breathing techniques, birth positions, pain management, breastfeeding basics, postpartum care
- Search via your Hebamme, hospital, or Geburtshaus

## The Hospital Birth (Klinikgeburt)

### Before labour:
- Register at your chosen Kreißsaal (delivery ward) around week 30–32
- Attend the Kreißsaal-Besichtigung (tour of the delivery ward)
- Know the hospital's phone number for the Kreißsaal — call before driving in when contractions start

### At the hospital:
- Bring your Mutterpass, insurance card (Krankenkasse), ID, and hospital bag
- A CTG trace (electronic fetal monitoring) will be done on arrival
- You have the right to an English-speaking midwife or interpreter at most university hospitals (Universitätsklinikum)

### Pain relief options in Germany:
- Epidural (Periduralanästhesie / PDA): widely available, must be requested in advance at some hospitals
- Gas and air (Lachgas): available in some clinics, not universal
- Water birth (Wassergeburt): available at many Kreißsäle and Geburtshäuser
- Acupuncture, TENS: often available

## Elterngeld (Parental Leave Benefit)

Germany provides one of Europe's most generous parental leave systems.

### Basiselterngeld:
- 65–67% of net income (max €1,800/month) for 12–14 months
- 2 "partner months" — father/partner must take at least 2 months to access the full 14 months
- Must apply within 3 months of birth via the local Elterngeldstelle

### ElterngeldPlus:
- Half the payment, twice as long (up to 28–32 months)
- Designed for part-time working parents

### Application:
- Apply at your local Elterngeldstelle (usually part of the Jugendamt)
- Required documents: birth certificate (Geburtsurkunde), income proof, employer confirmation

---

## Kindergeld (Child Benefit)

- €250/month per child (as of 2024), no income limit
- Apply at the Familienkasse (Federal Employment Agency)
- Application: formular.bafin.de or your local Agentur für Arbeit

---

## Krankenkasse (Health Insurance) for the Baby

- The baby must be registered with a health insurer within the first weeks after birth
- If both parents are in the gesetzliche Krankenversicherung (GKV), the baby is covered free via Familienversicherung
- Register by bringing the birth certificate to your Krankenkasse office

## Postpartum Support (Wochenbett)

The Wochenbett (the six weeks after birth) is taken seriously in Germany:

- **Hebamme home visits**: covered by insurance, available daily for the first 10 days, then as needed up to 8 weeks postnatally
- **Wochenbettdepression**: postnatal depression support is available via your GP, Hebamme, or psychotherapist. Do not suffer in silence — the German healthcare system covers mental health support
- **U1/U2/U3 examinations**: Mandatory paediatric check-ups for the baby at birth, day 3–5, and week 4–6 — bring the Kinderuntersuchungsheft (yellow child health booklet issued at birth)
