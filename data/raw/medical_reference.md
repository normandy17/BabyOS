# BabyOS Medical Reference Data

## Danger Signs — When to Go to Hospital Immediately

### Any Trimester — Call Emergency Services (112) or Go Immediately:
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking a pad in under an hour)
- Severe abdominal pain that does not ease
- High fever (above 38°C / 100.4°F) that does not respond to paracetamol
- Inability to keep any fluids down for 24+ hours
- Signs of anaphylaxis (swelling of face/throat, difficulty breathing)
- Loss of consciousness or seizure
- Sudden severe headache ("worst headache of my life")
- Visual disturbances: flashing lights, blurring, blind spots
- Sudden severe swelling of face, hands, or feet
- Chest pain or difficulty breathing

### First Trimester (Weeks 1–12):
- One-sided abdominal pain with or without bleeding (possible ectopic pregnancy — emergency)
- Shoulder tip pain (can indicate internal bleeding from ectopic)
- Passing tissue from the vagina
- Symptoms of miscarriage: persistent heavy bleeding with cramping

### Second Trimester (Weeks 13–27):
- Regular contractions before 24 weeks
- Sudden gush of fluid (possible premature rupture of membranes)
- Cervical pressure or feeling of something "coming out" (cervical incompetence)
- No fetal movement felt by 22 weeks

### Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40):
- Reduced or absent fetal movement (after 28 weeks, should feel 10 movements in 2 hours)
- Signs of preeclampsia: severe headache + visual changes + right upper abdominal pain + facial swelling
- Signs of preterm labour: regular contractions (every 5–10 minutes) before 37 weeks
- Waters breaking: clear fluid, greenish fluid, or foul-smelling fluid — go to hospital
- Placental abruption: sudden severe abdominal pain with or without visible bleeding
- Cord prolapse (emergency — cord visible at vaginal opening)

## Common Pregnancy Symptoms vs. When to Worry

| Symptom | Normal | Seek Help If... |
|---------|--------|----------------|
| Nausea and vomiting | Weeks 4–14 | Unable to keep fluids down for 24h, losing weight, feeling very unwell (hyperemesis gravidarum) |
| Mild cramping | Early pregnancy, third trimester Braxton Hicks | Cramping with heavy bleeding or fever at any stage |
| Backache | Throughout, especially third trimester | Severe, one-sided loin pain (could be kidney infection) |
| Vaginal discharge | Clear/white, increases through pregnancy | Yellow, green, smells bad, or causes itching/burning (infection) |
| Leg cramps | Second and third trimester | Redness, heat, swelling in one calf (possible DVT) |
| Headaches | Common in first and second trimester | Severe or persistent headache in third trimester (preeclampsia risk) |
| Tiredness | All of pregnancy | Extreme fatigue with breathlessness and pallor (anaemia) |
| Swollen feet | Third trimester, especially evenings | Sudden severe swelling of face and hands (preeclampsia) |
| Frequent urination | All of pregnancy | Burning or pain when urinating, fever (UTI) |
| Shortness of breath | Third trimester as uterus grows | Sudden onset, with chest pain or one-sided leg pain (PE risk) |

## Safe and Unsafe Foods in Pregnancy

### Foods to AVOID completely:
- Raw or undercooked meat (steak tartare, carpaccio, rare beef)
- Raw fish and shellfish (sushi, oysters, clams, mussels)
- Unpasteurised dairy (raw milk, some soft cheeses: Brie, Camembert, Roquefort, feta unless cooked)
- Liver and liver products (pâté, leberwurst) — excessive vitamin A
- High-mercury fish: shark, swordfish, tilefish, king mackerel, bigeye tuna
- Raw or partially cooked eggs (unless vaccinated hen eggs in UK/Germany)
- Sprouts (bean sprouts, alfalfa) — Listeria risk
- Unpasteurised juices
- Alcohol — no safe level in pregnancy
- Excess caffeine — limit to under 200mg/day (roughly 1 filter coffee or 2 espresso shots)

### Foods SAFE that are often wrongly avoided:
- Well-cooked eggs
- Well-cooked fish (salmon, cod, haddock, sardines — excellent for DHA)
- Hard cheeses (cheddar, gouda, parmesan)
- Pasteurised soft cheeses (mozzarella, cream cheese, cottage cheese)
- Peanuts (unless personal or family allergy history)
- Spicy food (does not harm the baby, may worsen heartburn)

### Particularly BENEFICIAL foods:
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale) — iron, folate, calcium
- Legumes (lentils, chickpeas, beans) — folate, iron, fibre
- Oily fish 2x/week (salmon, sardines, mackerel) — DHA omega-3 for brain development
- Dairy or fortified alternatives — calcium, vitamin D
- Lean red meat — iron, zinc, B12
- Eggs — protein, choline (important for brain development)
- Berries — antioxidants, vitamin C (enhances iron absorption)
- Whole grains — B vitamins, fibre
- Nuts and seeds — healthy fats, magnesium, vitamin E
- Avocado — folate, potassium, healthy fat

## Supplement Guide

| Supplement | Dose | Why | Start |
|-----------|------|-----|-------|
| Folic acid | 400mcg/day (standard) or 5mg/day (high-risk) | Neural tube defect prevention | At least 12 weeks before conception, continue to week 12 |
| Vitamin D | 10mcg (400 IU)/day | Bone development, immune function | Throughout pregnancy |
| Iron | 14.8mg/day from diet; supplement only if deficient (test first) | Oxygen transport, prevents anaemia | If blood tests show deficiency |
| Calcium | 1000mg/day from diet | Fetal bone and tooth development | Throughout |
| DHA (omega-3) | 200mg/day | Fetal brain and eye development | Second and third trimester especially |
| Iodine | 150–200mcg/day | Thyroid and brain development | Throughout (most prenatal vitamins contain this) |

**Do NOT take**: Extra vitamin A (above prenatal vitamin levels), St John's Wort, herbal supplements without medical approval.

## Common Tests and What They Mean

### First Trimester Blood Tests:
- **Blood group and Rhesus factor**: Determines if anti-D injections are needed
- **Full blood count (FBC)**: Checks for anaemia and infection
- **Rubella immunity**: If not immune, avoid contact with infected people
- **HIV test**: Routine in most countries; treatment prevents mother-to-child transmission
- **Hepatitis B and C**: Screening for management planning
- **Thyroid function (TSH)**: Thyroid problems affect pregnancy outcome
- **TORCH screen** (Toxoplasma, Others, Rubella, CMV, Herpes): In Germany often included

### First Trimester Screening (Weeks 11–14):
- **Nuchal Translucency (NT) scan**: Fluid at back of baby's neck; increased NT raises risk of chromosomal conditions
- **Combined screening**: NT + blood hCG + PAPP-A gives risk score for Down syndrome, Edwards syndrome, Patau syndrome
- **NIPT (non-invasive prenatal testing)**: Optional blood test, more accurate than combined screening; private in Germany unless high-risk

### Second Trimester:
- **Anomaly scan (20 weeks)**: Checks all major organs, placenta position, amniotic fluid
- **Glucose tolerance test (24–28 weeks)**: 75g glucose drink, blood at 1 and 2 hours — screens for gestational diabetes

### Third Trimester:
- **Group B Streptococcus (GBS) swab (35–37 weeks)**: If positive, antibiotics given in labour to prevent neonatal infection
- **CTG (Cardiotocography)**: Records fetal heart rate and uterine contractions; used from week 36+

## Gestational Diabetes — What It Means

Gestational diabetes (GDM) affects 5–10% of pregnancies. It occurs when pregnancy hormones interfere with insulin function.

**Diagnosis**: Glucose tolerance test > 92mg/dL fasting, >180mg/dL at 1 hour, or >153mg/dL at 2 hours.

**Management**:
1. Dietary changes first: reduce refined carbohydrates, eat smaller more frequent meals, increase fibre and protein
2. Blood glucose monitoring at home (fasting and 1–2 hours after meals)
3. Insulin injections if diet alone does not control levels
4. Increased monitoring of fetal growth (extra ultrasounds)
5. Birth plan adjustments (earlier induction often recommended)

**After birth**: GDM usually resolves. 50% risk of Type 2 diabetes within 10 years — lifestyle modification important.

## Preeclampsia — What It Means

Preeclampsia is a serious condition affecting blood pressure and organs, typically after 20 weeks.

**Signs and symptoms**:
- Blood pressure ≥ 140/90 mmHg on two occasions
- Protein in urine (proteinuria)
- Severe headache
- Visual disturbances (flashing lights, blurred vision)
- Pain below ribs on the right side
- Sudden severe swelling of face, hands, or feet
- Feeling generally unwell

**If suspected**: Go to hospital immediately. This is a medical emergency.

**Treatment**: Monitoring, antihypertensive medication, magnesium sulphate, and delivery of the baby (the only cure).

**Risk factors**: First pregnancy, multiple pregnancy, previous preeclampsia, obesity, age >40, diabetes, kidney disease, family history.
