# Frequently Asked Questions About Pregnancy

## General Pregnancy FAQs

**Q: How is pregnancy calculated — from conception or last period?**
A: Pregnancy is calculated from the first day of your last menstrual period (LMP), not from conception. This means by the time you get a positive test at week 4, the pregnancy is already counted as 4 weeks even though conception happened about 2 weeks earlier. The full term is 40 weeks from LMP, or approximately 38 weeks from conception.

**Q: What is the due date and how accurate is it?**
A: The due date (Estimated Due Date / EDD) is calculated as 40 weeks from the first day of your last period. Only about 5% of babies are born on their exact due date. Normal delivery is considered anywhere between 37 weeks (early term) and 42 weeks (post-term). The 12-week ultrasound provides the most accurate due date estimate.

**Q: Can I exercise during pregnancy?**
A: Yes, for most women regular moderate exercise is beneficial throughout pregnancy. Recommended activities include walking, swimming, prenatal yoga, cycling on a stationary bike, and low-impact aerobics. Avoid contact sports, activities with fall risk, scuba diving, and exercising flat on your back after the first trimester. Aim for 150 minutes of moderate activity per week. Always consult your doctor or midwife if you have complications.

**Q: Is it normal to not feel pregnant?**
A: Yes, especially in the early weeks and during the second trimester for some women. Not everyone experiences severe nausea or strong symptoms. A lack of symptoms does not mean anything is wrong. If you had symptoms that suddenly disappear in the first trimester, it is worth mentioning to your midwife, but gradual symptom improvement is also completely normal around weeks 10–14.

**Q: How much weight should I gain during pregnancy?**
A: Recommended weight gain depends on pre-pregnancy BMI:
- Underweight (BMI < 18.5): 12.5–18 kg
- Normal weight (BMI 18.5–24.9): 11.5–16 kg
- Overweight (BMI 25–29.9): 7–11.5 kg
- Obese (BMI ≥ 30): 5–9 kg
- Twin pregnancy (normal weight): 17–25 kg

**Q: Can I travel during pregnancy?**
A: Yes, the second trimester (weeks 14–28) is the safest and most comfortable time to travel. Most airlines allow travel up to 36 weeks with a singleton pregnancy, though some require a doctor's letter after 28 weeks. Long-haul flights carry a slightly increased DVT risk — stay hydrated, walk the aisle, and wear compression socks. Avoid travel to areas with Zika virus, malaria risk, or limited medical facilities.

**Q: Can I have sex during pregnancy?**
A: Yes, sex is safe throughout most pregnancies. The baby is protected by the amniotic sac, the uterine wall, and the mucus plug. Exceptions where your doctor may advise abstaining include: placenta praevia, risk of preterm labour, unexplained bleeding, or ruptured membranes. Many couples find their libido changes throughout pregnancy — all variations are normal.

## First Trimester FAQs

**Q: Is morning sickness dangerous?**
A: Typical morning sickness — nausea with or without vomiting, mainly in the first trimester — is not harmful to the baby and is actually associated with a lower miscarriage risk in some studies. However, hyperemesis gravidarum (HG), a severe form causing inability to keep any fluids or food down, leading to dehydration and weight loss, requires medical treatment including IV fluids and anti-nausea medication. Go to hospital if you cannot keep fluids down for more than 24 hours.

**Q: What causes miscarriage and can it be prevented?**
A: About 10–20% of known pregnancies end in miscarriage, the majority in the first trimester. Most first-trimester miscarriages are caused by chromosomal abnormalities in the embryo — a random error in cell division that cannot be prevented or caused by anything the parents did. They are not caused by exercise, sex, stress, arguments, lifting, or falling. Risk factors include age (especially over 35), certain medical conditions, and structural uterine abnormalities. Recurrent miscarriage (3 or more) warrants investigation.

**Q: When should I tell people I'm pregnant?**
A: This is entirely your choice. Many couples wait until after the 12-week scan when miscarriage risk drops significantly. Others tell close family immediately for support. There is no right or wrong time. Some couples in high-stress or demanding jobs choose to tell employers earlier. Consider who you would tell in the event of a miscarriage, as this can guide your decision about early disclosure.

**Q: Is spotting in early pregnancy normal?**
A: Light spotting (a few drops of pink or brown blood) in the first trimester can be normal, often caused by implantation bleeding or cervical sensitivity. However, any bleeding should be reported to your doctor or midwife. Heavy red bleeding with cramping should be assessed urgently as it can indicate miscarriage or ectopic pregnancy.

## Second Trimester FAQs

**Q: When will I feel the baby move?**
A: First-time mothers typically feel fetal movement (called quickening) between weeks 18–22. Women who have been pregnant before may feel it earlier, from around week 16. Early movements feel like flutters, bubbles, or light taps. By weeks 24–28, movement is stronger and more recognisable. After 28 weeks, you should be aware of a regular pattern of movement.

**Q: What is the anatomy scan and what does it check?**
A: The 20-week anatomy scan (also called the anomaly scan or Feindiagnostik in Germany) is the most comprehensive ultrasound of the pregnancy. It checks: brain structure, face (cleft lip detection), spine, heart (four chambers, valves, major vessels), lungs, abdominal organs (stomach, kidneys, bladder), limbs, placenta position, amniotic fluid levels, and fetal growth measurements. It cannot detect every abnormality, but identifies major structural issues. The sex of the baby can usually be determined at this scan.

**Q: What is gestational diabetes?**
A: Gestational diabetes (GDM) is a form of diabetes that develops during pregnancy, caused by pregnancy hormones impairing insulin function. It is diagnosed via the glucose tolerance test at 24–28 weeks. Most cases are managed with dietary changes; some require insulin. It usually resolves after birth but increases risk of Type 2 diabetes later. Babies of mothers with GDM may be larger (macrosomia) and need close monitoring.

**Q: What does placenta praevia mean?**
A: Placenta praevia means the placenta is positioned low in the uterus, partially or completely covering the cervix. It is detected at the 20-week scan. Many cases of low-lying placenta resolve by themselves as the uterus grows. True placenta praevia at term requires a planned caesarean section. It can cause painless bleeding in the second and third trimesters.

## Third Trimester FAQs

**Q: What are Braxton Hicks contractions?**
A: Braxton Hicks are irregular, practice contractions that prepare the uterus for labour. They are usually painless or mildly uncomfortable, irregular in timing, do not get progressively stronger, and ease with movement, rest, or a warm bath. They become more common and noticeable in the third trimester. They are different from true labour contractions, which are regular, rhythmic, progressive (get stronger and closer together), and do not stop with rest.

**Q: What is the mucus plug and what does losing it mean?**
A: The mucus plug seals the cervix during pregnancy, protecting the baby from infection. As the cervix begins to soften and dilate in preparation for labour, the plug dislodges. This can happen days or weeks before labour begins, or during early labour itself. It looks like a thick glob of mucus, sometimes tinged pink or brown with blood (this is called the "show"). Losing the mucus plug alone is not a sign to go to the hospital — wait for regular contractions or waters breaking.

**Q: How do I know if my waters have broken?**
A: The amniotic sac can rupture as a sudden large gush of clear fluid, or as a slow trickle. It may be hard to distinguish from urine leakage, which is also common in late pregnancy. If you suspect your waters have broken, contact your hospital or midwife. Note the time, the colour (should be clear or pale straw coloured; green or brown suggests meconium), and any odour. If the fluid is greenish, go to hospital immediately.

**Q: When should I go to hospital in labour?**
A: The general rule for first-time mothers is the 4-1-1 or 5-1-1 rule: contractions every 4–5 minutes, lasting 1 minute, for at least 1 hour. Call the hospital labour ward (Kreißsaal) before arriving — they will assess you over the phone and advise. Go immediately (or call 112) if: your waters break, there is heavy bleeding, you cannot feel the baby moving, or you feel something is seriously wrong.

**Q: What is a birth plan?**
A: A birth plan is a written summary of your preferences for labour and delivery. It communicates your wishes to midwives and doctors, especially if you cannot speak for yourself in the moment. Include: pain relief preferences (epidural yes/no/open), birth positions, who you want present, preferences for monitoring, feelings about interventions (episiotomy, forceps), cord clamping preferences (delayed cord clamping), skin-to-skin contact wishes, and feeding intentions. Keep it to one page. Understand that birth is unpredictable and flexibility is important.

**Q: What is an episiotomy?**
A: An episiotomy is a surgical cut made to the perineum (tissue between vagina and anus) to enlarge the vaginal opening during delivery. It was once routine but is now done selectively when the baby needs to be delivered quickly or if tearing seems likely to be severe. If you have strong feelings about this, include it in your birth plan. It is done under local anaesthetic and stitched after delivery.

## Postpartum FAQs

**Q: What are the baby blues?**
A: The baby blues are a brief period of emotional sensitivity, tearfulness, and mood swings that typically occur on days 3–5 after birth, when progesterone drops sharply. They affect up to 80% of new mothers and usually resolve within 2 weeks without treatment. This is different from postnatal depression (PND), which is more persistent and severe. If low mood, anxiety, or inability to cope persists beyond 2 weeks, speak to your Hebamme or GP.

**Q: When do periods return after birth?**
A: If bottle feeding, periods typically return 6–8 weeks after birth. If exclusively breastfeeding, the prolactin hormone suppresses ovulation, so periods may not return until breastfeeding reduces or stops — sometimes 6–12 months or longer. However, you can ovulate before your first period returns, meaning pregnancy is possible during breastfeeding without having had a period. Discuss contraception with your doctor at the postnatal check.

**Q: When is it safe to have sex after birth?**
A: Most healthcare providers advise waiting 6 weeks after birth, which coincides with the postnatal check. This allows physical healing — especially important after tearing, episiotomy, or caesarean section. Many women feel ready later than 6 weeks, and this is completely normal. Breastfeeding can cause vaginal dryness due to lower oestrogen; lubricant is recommended.

**Q: What is the U1 examination?**
A: The U1 (Untersuchung 1) is the first mandatory paediatric examination in Germany, performed by a doctor within minutes of birth. It assesses the APGAR score (breathing, heart rate, muscle tone, reflexes, colour), checks for birth injuries, congenital abnormalities, and basic reflexes. Results are recorded in the Kinderuntersuchungsheft (yellow booklet) which the hospital issues. Keep this booklet — it tracks all developmental examinations through childhood.
